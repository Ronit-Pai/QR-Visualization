How QR Codes Work

An illustrated guide to encoding, structure, and error correction

Quick Response (QR) codes are two-dimensional barcodes that can store text, URLs, or other data. From mobile payments to event tickets and Wi-Fi sharing, they've become part of daily life. But what's inside these black-and-white squares? Let's break it down step by step.

1) A Little History

QR codes were invented in 1994 by Denso Wave, a Japanese company, to track car parts. Their fast readability and large storage capacity soon made them popular worldwide. During the COVID-19 pandemic, QR codes became a safe way to share menus, check in to venues, and make contactless payments.

2) Structure of a QR Code

QR codes are grids of dark and light modules with special patterns that help scanners detect, align, and decode the data.

3) Data Encoding

Your text is converted into bits, then arranged into 8-bit codewords. Different modes affect capacity:

Codewords are interleaved to improve robustness against localized damage.

4) Error Correction (Reed-Solomon)

QR codes add parity codewords so that even if part of the code is damaged, the original data can be recovered. Levels L/M/Q/H provide 7-30% recoverability.

5) Scanning & Decoding

A scanner locates finder patterns, aligns perspective, reads timing patterns, then decodes the data and applies error correction.

  • Maximum capacity: 7,089 digits or 4,296 alphanumeric characters.
  • Thanks to error correction, QR codes can even include logos or designs without breaking.
